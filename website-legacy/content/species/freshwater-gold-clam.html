Freshwater gold clam is managed in Queensland as an established invasive pest. It was first detected in Australia at Colleges Crossing (west of Brisbane) in October 2023 — the first positive identification of this species in Australia. Surveillance has confirmed it along a 70 km+ stretch of the Brisbane River, from the upper reaches to below Colleges Crossing. Queensland works with partners across Australia and New Zealand on early detection, reporting, and preventing spread.

photo_library What it looks like

Also known as Asian clam, Asian golden clam, pygmy clam, Asiatic clam, prosperity clam, or golden clam. Clams range from pale yellow to gold but may also appear blackened. They have an obvious ribbed texture on the shell.

  • Inflated, round to triangular shell — yellowish brown to black with evenly spaced ridges
  • Usually under 25 mm; can grow to 50–65 mm shell length
  • Tolerates a wide variety of substrate and habitat types
  • Often on sandy or muddy river beds; may see shells or breathing tubes at the water’s edge

water Habitat and distribution

Optimal habitats are rivers with sandy bottoms and moderate flow, but the clam also lives in ponds, lakes, and rivers of all sizes with variable flows and sedimentation. It prefers sand and gravel but dense populations have been found on hard substrates including concrete and man-made structures, especially in well-oxygenated, fast-flowing water.

Known in Queensland: limited locations along the Brisbane River catchment in the Ipswich region, including:

  • Colleges Crossing
  • Savages Crossing
  • Riverside Park
  • Somerset Dam

It may be present in other waterways — when unsure, still report. The species is also established in New Zealand and parts of North and South America. Climate modelling suggests much of south-eastern Australia could be at risk if the clam spreads.

cycle Life cycle

  • Fertilised eggs form larvae that live in plankton for up to 55 days before settling
  • Generally thought to reproduce twice a year, depending on climate and ecology
  • Rapid growth rate and high reproductive rate — larvae can spread over a broad area via river flows
  • A single clam can release hundreds of juveniles per day; new populations can start from one individual

eco Why it harms biodiversity

Freshwater gold clam is a highly invasive species owing to its rapid growth, high reproductive rate, and ability to broadcast larvae using river flows. It is a long-distance hitchhiker in ballast water and through the aquarium trade.

Environmental impacts

  • Highly invasive — can displace native clams and diminish water quality
  • Competes with native freshwater bivalves for food and habitat
  • Larvae and juveniles spread downstream and can resuspend in water currents

Economic impacts

  • Can block plumbing, heat exchangers, and other water infrastructure
  • Overseas, invasion has cost billions in clogged water systems
  • Mud on boats, trailers, and shoes can spread it to new places

directions_boat How it spreads

  • Attached to boats or carried in ballast water
  • Used as bait or sold through the aquarium trade
  • Carried with water currents and river flows
  • Accidentally moved on fishing gear, waders, and trailers

Slipway operators, vessel inspectors, and vessel owners should thoroughly check, clean, and dry vessel hulls and niche areas such as internal bilge systems.

What to do

Do

  • Take a photo if safe
  • Note your location
  • Report to Biosecurity Queensland within 24 hours
  • Check, clean, dry boats, trailers, and fishing gear
  • Take reasonable steps to prevent spread until an authorised officer contacts you

Don’t

  • Pick up, move, keep, feed, give away, sell, or release the clam
  • Move water or mud to another river
  • Use as bait or in aquariums

Under the Biosecurity Act 2014, freshwater gold clam is category 2, 3, 4 and 5 restricted matter. Penalties may apply if you move, keep, or release it.

Look-alikes — native Corbicula australis

The invasive clam looks very similar to the native Australian species Corbicula australis, which lives in river systems throughout mainland Australia including the Brisbane River. Because of this similarity, identification should be done by a trained expert. If you are not sure, still report — never move what you find.

Specimens showing heavy concentric ridges and colour variation — © Queensland Government (DPI gallery)

  • C. fluminea (pest): thicker, more triangular shell; heavier ridges with wider spacing (7–14 ribs per cm); can reach 65 mm; often in higher densities
  • C. australis (native): thinner, more oval shell; finer, closely spaced ridges (25–32 ribs per cm); rarely exceeds 33 mm

manage_accounts Official management

Queensland manages freshwater gold clam through surveillance, community reporting, and best-practice decontamination — aligned with national guidelines. Eradication is not considered technically feasible; the focus is on slowing spread and protecting river health. Boating and fishing communities, aquarium retailers, and local governments all play a role in prevention.
